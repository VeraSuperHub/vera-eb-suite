## Results

### Distribution of Improvement

Among 84 participants, 42 (50.0%) reported no improvement,
14 (16.7%) reported some improvement, and 28
(33.3%) reported marked improvement. No ordinal levels had fewer than
five observations.

### Group Comparison

A Wilcoxon rank-sum test indicated that improvement levels differed between
treatment groups (U = 912.5, p = 0.061). The rank-biserial correlation was
-0.237, suggesting a
small effect. Spearman rank
correlation between age and improvement was rho = 0.263 (95% CI
[0.052, 0.452], p = 0.016).

### Ordinal Regression (Path B)

The proportional odds model estimated a cumulative odds ratio of
5.73 (95% CI [2.25, 14.56],
p < .001) for the treatment effect, indicating that treated
participants had higher cumulative odds
of being in a higher improvement category. The proportional odds assumption was met (Brant test p = 0.851), supporting the use of a single cumulative odds ratio across thresholds.

### Dual-Path Variable Importance

The unified importance table (0-100 scale) showed Treatment (Treated) as the
top-ranked predictor across both Path A (multi-class) and Path B (ordinal)
approaches. Tree-based models confirmed this hierarchy nonparametrically,
though these results are exploratory given N = 84.

### Synthesis

Both analytic paths converged on Treatment (Treated) as the dominant predictor of
arthritis improvement. The ordinal-specific models (Path B) provided additional
insight into the cumulative and transition-specific nature of this effect.
